With decades of scholarship in corporate governance, human rights, and business ethics — and a legal career spanning corporate law, federal testimony, and expert witness work in high-profile cases — Professor Santoro brings the precise expertise to address the most pressing question asked today: who is accountable when AI systems fail?

Now, he helps governments, institutions, and legal practitioners close the gap between where AI decisions are made and where accountability is triggered — challenging the prevailing assumption of human oversight after deployment. His work argues that accountability must be structured into AI systems at the point of design and delegation.

Consulting & Expert Witness

Professor Santoro’s consulting practice draws on over 40 years of direct observation across factory floors in China, corporate boardrooms, and research laboratories.

He has served as an expert witness in high-profile federal cases — advising on corporate governance reforms at Merck following the Vioxx scandal, and testifying on institutional accountability in the Wells Fargo fake-account litigation. In both cases, his work helped shape the governance frameworks adopted as part of settlement agreements.

He advises on AI governance, corporate ethics, and human rights compliance.

About

Professor Michael A. Santoro studies how ethical and legal duties are made enforceable — through governance, institutional design, and accountability mechanisms that function at scale. His current work focuses on artificial intelligence: who is responsible when AI systems fail, how accountability can be structured upstream before deployment, and what democratic institutions must do to govern AI in the public interest.

Working at the cutting edge of AI accountability and management, his current work builds on decades of scholarship in governance, accountability, and human rights — fields where the gap between ethical principle and enforceable practice has long been his central concern.

His work is guided by a simple premise: ethical principles matter only insofar as they are transparent, equitable, and enforceable.
